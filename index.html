FGGS-LiDAR: Ultra-Fast, GPU-Accelerated Simulation from General 3DGS Models to LiDAR

Junzhe Wu1, Yufei Jia2, Yiyi Yan3, Zhixing Chen1, Tiao Tan1, Zifan Wang4, Guangyu Wang1, BoKui Chen1†, Guyue Zhou5†

Abstract

FGGS-LiDAR bridges general 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) assets and high-performance LiDAR simulation. We introduce a prior-free 3DGS-to-mesh pipeline based on volumetric discretization and narrow-band TSDF, producing watertight, topologically consistent meshes from arbitrary pretrained 3DGS models—without LiDAR-specific supervision or architectural changes. Paired with a fully GPU-accelerated, BVH-based ray-casting engine, our simulator renders first-hit ranges at 500+ FPS on million-triangle scenes while preserving geometric fidelity.

On indoor/outdoor benchmarks, our simulations closely match scans rendered from real-scanned meshes, enabling plug-and-play reuse of existing 3DGS assets for scalable, multi-modal simulation.

Method

Pipeline Overview

The FGGS-LiDAR pipeline integrates Gaussian-based scene representation with geometric reconstruction and sensor simulation. It first converts a pretrained 3DGS model into voxelized occupancy fields, followed by TSDF fusion to reconstruct watertight surfaces. A GPU-based BVH acceleration structure enables real-time ray traversal for LiDAR simulation, achieving sub-millisecond per-frame rendering. This unified design bridges 3D Gaussian representations and high-fidelity LiDAR perception for downstream embodied tasks.

Scene Rendering

Our reconstructed meshes preserve the photometric and geometric details of the original 3DGS scenes. Compared with direct Gaussian splat rendering, the mesh-based representation provides consistent global structure, sharper boundaries, and accurate surface normals, which are essential for physically grounded LiDAR ray interactions. The resulting renderings demonstrate that our conversion process maintains fidelity while enabling real-time simulation.

3DGS Voxelization

We voxelize the continuous Gaussian fields into discrete occupancy volumes using an LBVH-accelerated accumulation process. Each Gaussian contributes probabilistically to local density fields, and a sparse voxel grid is efficiently updated on the GPU. This step captures fine geometric structures while maintaining high computational throughput, serving as the foundation for TSDF-based surface recovery.

Denoised → Mesh

To produce clean and watertight surfaces, we apply narrow-band TSDF propagation and surface extraction via Marching Cubes. The resulting meshes are further refined by structure-preserving smoothing to remove local artifacts without shrinking geometry. This ensures physically valid, topologically consistent meshes suitable for ray-based simulation and robotic perception benchmarks.

Depth Limitations

Depth-based representations often suffer from missing regions, discontinuities, and unstable edges, especially under occlusion or reflective surfaces. Such artifacts lead to inaccurate geometry and hinder physically consistent simulation. Our approach overcomes these limitations by reconstructing continuous, watertight surfaces that preserve fine details and eliminate depth ambiguity.

Results

LiDAR FPS vs Triangle Count

FGGS-LiDAR achieves stable real-time simulation performance across a wide range of mesh complexities. Even at multi-million triangle scales, our BVH-based ray traversal maintains near-logarithmic time complexity and consistent frame rates above 500 FPS. This enables efficient large-scale experiments with minimal computational overhead.

LiDAR Throughput vs Triangle Count

The GPU-parallelized LiDAR simulator reaches an effective throughput exceeding 7.5×107 points per second on commodity hardware. Through optimized memory layout and warp-coherent traversal, the system maintains high efficiency while scaling gracefully with scene complexity. This throughput level supports training and evaluation of data-hungry embodied AI systems with realistic LiDAR feedback.
